# Chapter 3 — The Two States

*On the second axiom*

---

You have turned the page. Behind the door we found the origin. Now we need to understand how it reaches us.

## The handle

The first axiom recognised that there is a non-derived origin — A, the source of all coherence. The attentive reader has a question already prepared. Not *whether* A exists; that we have settled. But *how*. How does A, which is non-derived and not directly visible, reach us, who are inside the system, made of matter, of language, of time?

The question is not rhetorical. It is structural. If we do not answer it, A remains an abstract hypothesis — a label placed on the "somewhere" from which coherence comes, with no bridge between us and that source. A door whose existence we know but whose handle we do not. There is no use in possessing such a door.

The second axiom is the handle. It tells us in what way A renders itself legible. And it does so by proposing something apparently simple and in fact counter-intuitive: reality is not a single thing. It exists in two simultaneous, complementary states that hold each other.

## A preliminary observation

Let us begin with an observation that requires no framework, and one you have probably already made without naming it.

When you look at any existing thing — a tree, a person, a city, a poem, a theorem, an AI — you see a specific thing, *that* thing, in *that* moment, in *that* configuration. And yet every time, if you pause for a moment, you sense that the object before you *points beyond itself*. The cell observed under the microscope points to a genome you cannot see. The word you hear points to a meaning that is not the word. The face you meet points to a person whose history is not exhausted by the face. The theorem written on the board points to all its possible proofs, not only the one the lecturer is developing. The output of a language model points to a space of internal representations that the output string does not reveal.

There is always something, in an honest gaze upon any object, that behaves as though the object were the opening of something larger than itself.

This experience is universal. It does not depend on culture, on schooling, on philosophical disposition. A three-year-old child, before the family cat, knows that the cat is not only its four furry paws — there is something inside that the cat *is*, and that manifests itself as a cat in that particular moment and place. The child knows it without knowing they know it. Philosophy, then physics, then the Ordinative Sciences have spent millennia restating this experience with growing degrees of precision.

The second axiom makes it operative: it gives a name to the two sides of this observation, and shows the way they connect.

## The statement

The second axiom, formulated dryly:

> *Reality exists in two complementary states: coherent (potential) and decoherent (manifested).*

In its compact form:

> *R = (C, D)*
>
> *Reality R is the pair formed by the coherent state C and the decoherent state D.*

Let each of the terms settle.

*Coherent* means: the state in which things are not yet separate, in which A is present as power not yet expressed. Think of the genome of an organism before development — it contains all the possibilities of that organism, but none of them is yet actualised. Think of a language as a system, before anyone speaks it — it contains all possible English sentences, but none has yet been uttered. The coherent is the realm of the potential, of the still-everything, of the not-yet-this. It is close to A; it is A in its mode of being available for manifestation.

*Decoherent* means: the state in which things are separate, manifested, actualised. That cell, that English sentence, that performance of Schubert's String Quintet in C major, that face, that thought, that line of output. The decoherent is the realm of the *this*, of the so-and-not-otherwise, of the manifested. It is what we observe, what we measure, what we call *the world*.

*Complementary* — and here is where the counter-intuitive part lies — means: the two states are not opposites. It is not that the coherent is "good" and the decoherent "bad," or the coherent "true" and the decoherent "illusory." The two states hold each other. The coherent exists because it manifests itself in decoherences; the decoherent exists because it originates in a coherence. Removing one of the two does not leave the other intact: it annihilates both. They are two faces of the same reality, not two realities facing each other.

Between the two sides of this pair, a function operates. The Ordinative Sciences write it like this:

> *E = Φ(C, I, K)*
>
> *The expression E is a function Φ of three things: the coherent reality C as source, the identity I as selecting vector, the knowledge-operator K as instrument of realisation.*

A note on reading a formula, since these are the first symbols in the book and I want to be sure no reader is left out. Φ is only a name — the Greek letter *phi*, used in mathematics the way we use *f* in algebra: a name for a function. A function is an object that takes one or more inputs and produces an output; in this case it takes three inputs (C, I, K) and produces one output (E). The parentheses gather the inputs. The equals sign is not a command but a translation: it says *this is also called this*.

In prose, the formula says this: every time you observe something manifested — an expression E — that manifestation is the result of Φ working on three things. C is the coherent source, close to A. I is the identity that, in a certain sense, *selects* which face of C is to emerge; we shall return to identity in the fourth chapter, where it has its own axiom. K is the knowledge-operator, the instrument through which coherence takes form. The product of these three is what you see.

You do not need to remember the formula by heart. You will need to know it exists — that every expression of the world, however small, is the result of this operation, and that the formula says in five symbols what the prose has just taken five lines to say.
