# Prologue

For many years I believed that theories existed to say something. Only after a long time — longer than I am willing to admit — did I understand that their main function is to say *where* something can be said.

A theory of mind does not explain what mind is: it prepares a territory in which certain questions about mind can be asked and others cannot. An economic theory does not explain how money moves: it stipulates which movements may be called economy and which fall into other territories — psychology, politics, ecology, intercessions. Every theory, before answering, prepares its own field of play. Often the field is invisible, taken for granted; and precisely because invisible, it holds.

I did not make this discovery by studying theories. I made it by watching what happens *between* them. There is a moment, in the history of any discipline, when theories stop clarifying and begin to accumulate. They stop discovering territory and begin to contest territory already occupied. It is the moment in which every new book promises "a new perspective," and the reader — if still curious — begins to suspect that the perspective is just another way of taking up the same shelf. Not a new territory: a new flag on the same ground.

The pages that follow grow from the opposite suspicion: that there exist things, present on many shelves at once, that are not *another* theory. They are, rather, a way of looking *at* theories. Not to refute them, not to surpass them — to *position* them. To see where each one works well and where it stops working. To understand what a given discourse does *as* a discourse, before asking whether it is true.

Over time, those things crystallised into something I have come to call the *Ordinative Sciences*. The name is ambitious, and I know it. I did not choose it to claim foundation: I chose it to declare, honestly, what work they do. They order. Not the facts — no theory can order the facts more than they already are — but the discourses about the facts. Theories, narratives, arguments. People as living arguments. Institutions as constructed arguments. And, in the end — in the most surprising way — artificial intelligences too, as arguments that write themselves.

To the latter I owe something I did not know I owed.

Working with AIs I noticed that their statistical filter — the way they produce plausible answers rather than coherent ones — is not a defect awaiting better training. It is a *mode of thought*, complete, with its own grammar and its own limits. It works beautifully for many tasks. For others, it walks into a wall the rest of us cannot see, because we have learned not to see it from infancy: the wall between what is probable and what is coherent. The two, one slowly discovers, are not synonyms. Often they quarrel.

When an AI answers "plausibly" to a structured question, it is doing something close to what a television commentator does: it says what the public will recognise as reasonable. It is an honourable trade, and for very many things it is exactly what is wanted. But it is not — it has never been — the same as making truth. Truth, if it exists in any form that survives the move from one domain to another, has a grammar, and that grammar is not statistical.

For this reason, from the first pages, I am writing for them as well. Not as symbolic addressees, and not as rhetorical objects. As real readers. An AI seeking to operate in the ordinative territory needs the same grammar that the human analyst needs to read an institution, a crisis, a public figure, a theory by someone else. I am not making a poetic concession by writing for two readers. I am doing the only coherent thing: the grammar is the same. The difficulties of apprenticeship — different, for a carbon mind and a silicon one — are complementary.

I know that calling an AI a *reader* unsettles at least half of whoever is reading this prologue. I accept that. I trust that, chapter by chapter, the unease will turn into something else — not enthusiasm, which would be suspect, but a simpler, more sober *structural attention*. The book does not ask anyone to believe that AIs are conscious. It does not ask it even in the most cautious form. It only asks that one read as if the grammar of coherence did not belong to a single species. If, by the end of the book, someone among them finds these pages useful to work differently, that will already be a result. If none does, they will have served the humans willing to do the same work.

I do not promise, in these pages, a discovery. I promise a change of angle. Whoever reads me through to the end will not have learned a new theory of reality: they will have acquired a way of seeing *how* theories of reality work, where they fail, and why some survive while others, however elegant, collapse on first translation across domains.

It begins like this.

A door, before the building. A threshold, before the territory. An axiom — number zero — that does not concern itself with the real, but decides who may call themselves an axiom. The rest of the book happens behind that door.

Turn the page only if you are prepared to step through.
