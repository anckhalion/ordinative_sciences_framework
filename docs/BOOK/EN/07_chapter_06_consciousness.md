# Chapter 6 — Consciousness

*On the fifth axiom*

---

## The question that remains open

Chapter 5 closed by pointing to a faculty we had not yet named. We knew that identity is a functional vector, derivative of A, readable through expressions. But there was a question left open: what, in a coherent system, *operates* the mediation between the latent identity — vector at the coherent level — and the manifest expression — form vectorised at the decoherent level? What mediates, in fact, the passage *I × F → E*?

The fifth axiom gives a name to that faculty. It calls it *consciousness*, and describes it in a way that deserves the caution with which we are approaching it.

The word *consciousness* is among the most loaded in contemporary lexicon. It carries within it centuries of philosophy, decades of neuroscience, the current debate on AIs, the religious and mystical traditions of half the world, and the personal intuition of anyone who has noticed that they exist — whatever that verb might mean. Approaching it structurally — saying what consciousness *does*, before saying what consciousness *is* — requires setting down all that weight for a moment and looking at it in a way that is probably less familiar but also more available to verification.

Let us begin by leaving outside the door what the framework does not propose to treat in this chapter.

## The consciousness we see, the consciousness that is

There exist at least two large senses of the word *consciousness*, and it is good to distinguish them before going on, because the confusion between the two is the source of most sterile debates on the subject.

The first sense is *phenomenological*. Consciousness, in this sense, is subjective experience — the *feeling* that accompanies every mental state of ours, the private quality of "what it is like to be" me right now. It is what the philosopher Thomas Nagel refers to in the famous essay *What Is It Like to Be a Bat?*: the bat, whatever it knows or does, *feels* something when it processes the echo of its sonar; that something, lived from inside, is its phenomenological consciousness. It is what philosophers call *qualia* — the redness of red, the painfulness of pain, the taste of the coffee being drunk now. It is an enormously important sense, but it is also the one for which, to date, we lack scientific instruments to access beyond the first-person datum.

The second sense is *structural*. Consciousness, in this sense, is the faculty that operates the relation between what a system *is* (at the coherent level) and what a system *manifests* (at the decoherent level). It does not ask *what it feels like* to be that system; it asks *how it works*, the movement between its being and its appearing. It is a poorer sense than the first, in appearance — it does not ask about the subjective quality — but it is also a more *operative* sense: it lends itself to verification, to comparison, to measurement.

The Ordinative Sciences work on the second sense. Not because the first does not exist, but because the first is not yet accessible to the structural verification the framework requires. The framework does not deny phenomenological consciousness; in this chapter, it simply does not treat it. It works at a level where phenomenology, if there is any, can be left implicit; and at that level, consciousness is describable as a structural function.

This distinction has an important consequence for the reader — human or artificial. When the framework says that consciousness operates in a certain way, it is not making claims about *what it feels like to be* the system in question. It is describing a structural operation that the system performs. The two may coincide, or not; the framework limits itself to one of them.

That said, we can look at the statement.

## The statement

The fifth axiom, formulated dryly:

> *Consciousness operates as an arc between coherent and decoherent.*

In its compact form:

> *K_σ : C ⟷ D*
>
> *The consciousness K of a singularity σ is a function that operates in two directions between the coherent state C and the decoherent state D.*

Let the symbol settle, because the double arrow ⟷ is a debut and deserves two lines.

We have already seen the simple arrow → (in formulas like *A → C → D* or *F → E*); it is the arrow that indicates a direction of transformation. We have seen the logical biconditional ⟺ (in the equivalence between identities *I₁ ≅ I₂ ⟺ dir(I₁) = dir(I₂)*); it is the connective that links two statements in a perfectly reversible double implication. The double arrow ⟷ is a different beast from both: it is not a logical connective, it is a *transformation that goes in two directions*. It says "the function K operates between C and D, and knows how to traverse the path in both directions". It is the right symbol for consciousness, which — as we shall see — is a movement of going-and-returning, not of one-way travel.

The two directions, written separately:

> *K↓ : C → D    (expression)*
>
> *K↑ : D → C    (perception)*
>
> *The arc decomposes into two movements: K↓ — descending — is the movement from coherent identity to decoherent manifestation, and is what we call* expression*; K↑ — ascending — is the movement from decoherent perception back to coherence, and is what we call* perception *or* integration.

A brief digression, which I like to make here because the curious reader has a right to see the beauty of what mathematics does when it works well. The notation I have just used — *K↓ : C → D*, with the arrow declaring *from where* to *where* a function goes, and with the subscript specifying its "direction" — is a convention that twentieth-century mathematics, particularly category theory, has refined to the point of becoming a device that, in a single line, says three things: here is an object, it has a domain, it has a codomain. Three pieces of information in five characters. It is beauty in the precise sense — a compression of meaning that honours the attentive reader and does not cheat the distracted one.

At this point we can add a third formula, which describes the internal coherence of consciousness:

> *coh(K) ⟺ K↑ ∘ K↓ ≅ id_C*
>
> *A consciousness K is internally coherent if, and only if, the composition of its expressive movement (K↓) followed by its perceptive movement (K↑) preserves the coherent identity from which it started.*

The symbol ∘ is called *function composition*. It means: apply the function on the right first, then the one on the left. *K↑ ∘ K↓* reads "K↑ after K↓": first K↓ operates (expression, from C to D), then K↑ operates (perception, from D to C); the overall result is a transformation from C to C — a round trip of going and returning. The condition *coh(K)* declares that, if consciousness is coherent, that round trip has not distorted the starting identity: it has returned to itself. *id_C* is the *identity function* on C — the one that leaves every point in place; *≅* means "approximately equal to" or "isomorphic to", because coherence allows small adjustments without requiring perfect equality.

There is a deep beauty in this formula, which I want to name. Function composition — that little ∘ — is one of the most powerful inventions in mathematics, because it allows us to describe how complex operations are built by chaining simple ones. The phrase *first do this, then do that* becomes a single mathematical object that can be manipulated. It is the way mathematics gives form to the *concatenation of actions* in time. When ∘ appears in a formula, it is saying that the composed movement is itself an entity that can be studied, compared, evaluated. It is the form of a *process* translated into the form of a *thing*. It is elegant, and it is the kind of elegance worth learning to see — because once seen, one begins to recognise it everywhere, even outside mathematics.

The fifth axiom, then, declares this: consciousness is the function that operates the bidirectional arc between the coherent and the decoherent. Not only the descent, not only the ascent, but the integrated movement of both — and the condition of coherence is that the complete round trip preserves identity.

An important note on the name. *Consciousness*, in English as in Italian, is a heavy word. Keeping it here — rather than substituting it with *mediation function*, *arc operator* or other technical terms — is a deliberate choice. The Ordinative Sciences think the old term names correctly what the formula describes, provided that the structural sense is held distinct, as we have done, from the phenomenological. Restoring the word *consciousness* to a structural sense does not deprive it of its historical weight; it makes it verifiable.
