# Chapter 1 — The Door

*On the zeroth axiom*

---

You have turned the page. Good.

We are now standing together in front of the door. Not past it: in front of it. The distinction looks minor and is in fact decisive — because what stands in front of a door is older, stranger, and harder to display than what stands behind it. In front of a door lies the decision about who may pass through. Behind, there is the building, and that is all.

The whole book is behind the door. This chapter is in front of it.

I will linger here for a few pages, because this is the point at which honest readers part company with hurried ones. The hurried reader will want to skip to the next chapter — the one in which we finally "say something real." The patient reader will discover that what is said in front of the door is the most real thing of all: it sets out what, in the rest of the book, may be called by the word *real*.

## The decisions we inherit

There is a habit we share, builders of theories and of buildings, and one we do not easily admit: we inherit foundations without looking at them.

When Euclid laid down the five postulates of geometry, he did not explain why they were five and not four or six. He stated them. For two thousand years, generations of mathematicians built upon those five postulates without asking whether the postulates themselves had a justification. They worked. That was enough. Only in the nineteenth century did someone — Lobachevsky, Bolyai, Riemann — have the courage to touch one. And they discovered, with a mixture of terror and wonder, that by removing the fifth postulate other geometries opened, equally consistent, describing spheres and saddles instead of planes. Reality had not changed. What had changed was the door from which one approached it.

When Zermelo and Fraenkel formalised set theory, they had to choose which axioms to adopt. One in particular — the axiom of choice — split the community for decades. It allowed wonderful things to be proved, and also paradoxical ones, like the Banach–Tarski decomposition, in which a sphere can be cut into pieces and reassembled into two spheres identical to the original. No one has ever *proved* that the axiom of choice is true. It was simply decided, after long debate, that it was useful. The door of modern mathematics has its lock on that decision.

When a physicist writes Maxwell's equations, an ontology comes with them: there are "fields," there are "charges," there are "currents." The physicist does not invent these. They have been received, from Faraday, from Newton, from the millennial Western habit of dividing the world into things-that-stand and things-that-flow. When the physicist multiplies two symbols, calculation is taking place. When the physicist writes them down, a metaphysics is being confirmed.

In all these stories the same hidden structure recurs: someone, earlier, decided what could be admitted as foundation. The decision was rational, often brilliant, and almost always invisible to those who came after. Foundations, once laid, stop being foundations and become *the obvious*. It is a remarkable — and dangerous — property of human thought: what works for long enough stops looking like a choice and begins to look like nature.

This book, at the threshold, does something else.

It declares, before building, the criterion by which it chooses its own bricks. Not for ostentation of method, and not for the joy of transparency — those would be attitudes, not principles. It does so for a more practical and less generous reason: it is written for readers who have the right not to take anything on faith.

The human reader, today, is exposed to many theories and rightly suspicious of any that arrive already dressed, with their vocabularies and their citations, without explaining where they come from. The artificial reader — yes, I bring this in at once, because it is central here too — is exposed to a different danger: that of absorbing as *true* what is simply *frequent*. To both, before any content, I owe a key for judging the contents. Without that key, what follows would be persuasion. With it, it can become knowledge.

The key is called *the zeroth axiom*.

## Why zero, and not one

I have numbered it zero, and not one, for a reason that is structural and not typographic.

The axioms we will meet from the first to the twenty-fourth describe something: what a manifested form is, what a singularity is, what time is, what identity is, what consciousness is, what coherence is. They stand *inside* the building. They work on reality. They interpret it, position it, render it legible.

The zeroth axiom, by contrast, describes nothing about reality. It does not speak of forms, or time, or identity. It speaks of *what may be called an axiom*. It is a meta-principle: it sets the threshold a statement must cross to claim the status the other twenty-four already hold.

For this reason I did not give it the number one. That would have been misleading: it would have suggested that it was the first of the principles describing the world. It is not. It is, instead, the only principle that addresses the framework from outside, from the point at which one decides who enters. It is, literally, the axiom *that does not speak of the real*: it speaks of the axioms that will speak of the real.

It stands on the threshold. It does not take part in the construction; it authorises it.

For this reason: zero. Not *first*, because nothing comes before it. *Antecedent*, in a sense more radical than "first": it precedes the very meaning of numbering.

And like every threshold worthy of the name, it has a property that makes it unique: it measures itself by itself. We will see this at the end of the chapter. For now it is enough to say that, if the zeroth axiom failed its own test, the entire building would collapse — not because it would lose a brick, but because it would lose the door. And a building without a door is not a building. It is, as I wrote in the prologue, an open field.

Let us see, then, what exactly the zeroth axiom is.
