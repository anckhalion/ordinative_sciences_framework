# Chapter 4 — The Form

*On the third axiom*

---

## A word held in suspense

Chapter 3 left a word with its meaning suspended: *form*. We used it by saying that what we see in the decoherent state is a *manifested form*, but we did not specify what, exactly, a form is.

The imprecision was deliberate. I wanted the word to reach the reader with its ordinary meaning, before the framework took it back and made it technical. Now we can look at it.

The third axiom is its precision. It says something apparently obvious and in fact counter-intuitive: *every manifested form is an expression*. Not an autonomous object; not a thing-in-itself; not a neutral datum. An expression. Always, of something.

To understand why this sentence is less banal than it sounds, we first need to dissolve an old equivocation that the common lexicon has carried with it for centuries.

## Form and content: a distinction, not a separation

In everyday language we treat *form* and *content* as if they were two distinct and separable things. We speak of "the content of a book" as if it were something the book contains, and of "the form of a book" as if it were its envelope. The two seem separable even to thought: one could imagine the same content in another form — the same story in a film rather than in a novel, the same theorem in equations rather than in prose, the same message in a tweet rather than in a letter. The separation seems obvious.

At the practical level, it is. Literary criticism, music theory, the philosophy of art have built centuries of fruitful work on this distinction. Whoever analyses a sonnet may legitimately speak of its formal choices — the metre, the rhymes, the figures of speech — as distinct from its content — love, loss, the observation of the world. The framework does not come to take that instrument away from these disciplines.

What the framework says is something more subtle. The distinction between form and content is *operative* — useful for analysis, legitimate in practical discourse — but it is not *structural*. At the structural level, a form and its content are two aspects of the same thing, not two things. A form without content does not exist — it is an empty shell, and an empty shell is not a form, it is only a shell. A content without form does not exist — it is a potentiality that has not yet been stabilised, and so long as it is not stabilised it is not yet the content of anything: it is still pure coherence.

Some traditions have always known this. Aristotle, with his doctrine of *matter and form* — *hylomorphism* — held that every substance is simultaneously matter and form, inseparable: there is no matter without form, and no form without matter. Ancient poetics and rhetoric did not conceive of a *what is said* independent of the *how it is said*. Certain Eastern mystical and poetic traditions held the two aspects united for millennia, regarding any separation as an analytic device, never a truth about the thing. The framework does not stand in conflict with these traditions: it recognises their structural soundness, and finds itself among their *companions on the road*.

What the framework confronts — diagnosing, not polemicising — is a more recent inheritance: the cultural drift by which, in many areas of contemporary discourse, form has come to overwhelm content to the point of rendering it almost irrelevant. A piece of news matters for how it has been "presented", not for what it says; an idea is valued for how it is "communicated", not for its substance; a person is judged by their "image", not by what they do. It is a complicated story — of marketing, of media, of communication technology — and it is not the object of this book to recount it in full. It is enough to note it, because it is the reason the third axiom sounds, today, more radical than it would have sounded two thousand years ago. The third axiom puts the two things back into balance. Not from nostalgia for the traditions that held them united, but because the structure, when looked at honestly, requires it.

## The statement

The third axiom, formulated dryly:

> *Every manifested form is an expression.*

At this point it is worth fixing a distinction the statement leaves implicit: *form* and *expression*, in the framework, are not synonyms. They are different levels of one and the same chain.

Form is the stabilisation of a content into a recognisable configuration that is still *pre-contextual*. Expression is form placed in a specific context, with a vector and an orientation. The chain has three steps:

> *C → F → E*
>
> *Content stabilises into form; form vectorises into expression.*

The two transitions can be written separately:

> *F = stab(C)*
>
> *Form is the stabilisation of content.*

> *E = vect(F, I, K)*
>
> *Expression is the vectorisation of form through an identity I and a knowledge-operator K.*

The compact formula we already met in Chapter 3 — *E = Φ(C, I, K)* — is the composition of the two steps: from content directly to expression. Φ contains together the stabilisation (C → F) and the vectorisation (F → E).

We can say the same with a quantifier — a logical symbol worth introducing here for the first time:

> *∀ f ∈ D,  ∃ c ∈ C :  f = Φ(c, i, k)*
>
> *For every form f in the decoherent state, there exists a content c in the coherent state such that f is the product of the expression of c, given an identity i and an operator k.*

A note on ∀ and ∃, since they are symbols many readers see for the first time. The symbol ∀ — an upside-down *A* — means "for every", "whatever it may be", "in all cases"; the symbol ∃ — a backwards *E* — means "there exists", "there is at least one". The two symbols are called *quantifiers*, and they were born in logic and mathematics to say precisely things that in ordinary language would require many words. The formula above says, in ten symbols, what in English we would have had to write like this: *take any manifested form in the world, and you can be sure that behind it, a coherent content exists of which it is the expression*.

The difference between form and expression is best seen in two examples.

The lion, as *Panthera leo* — the form of the lion — exists before any particular lion. The child who learns the word *lion* in a picture-book, the adult who recognises a lion in a documentary, the poet who uses *lion* as a metaphor for courage, all access the same form. But when a specific lion, in a specific savanna, at dawn, approaches its prey — that is an expression of the lion: form vectorised in a context, with an orientation, with a specific state of the whole ensemble.

The verb *to be* has a form that exists before any of its specific occurrences: a grammar, a semantic structure, a position in the language, a network of possible meanings. When I say *I am tired*, *to be* expresses itself with a precise vector — first person, present, assertion of state. The form of *to be* preceded the sentence; the expression is *to be* contextualised, vectorised, situated.

The magic of expression, in both examples, is that it carries within itself *together* the content (the meaning of *lion*, of *to be*), the form (the recognisable stabilisation), the vector (the contextual direction), the context (the situation in which it occurs), and the state of the ensemble — the overall configuration of ⟨Σ, R, Φ⟩ in which the expression takes place. The expression is the synthesis unit: the richest, the most complete, the most informative of all the units the framework describes. This is why ordinative analysis always begins with an expression: it is there that all levels are co-present, and from there one can ascend — to the vector, to the form, to the content.

To fix the three terms, before going on:

*Content* — not "meaning" in the ordinary sense of paraphrase; it is the specific coherence that precedes stabilisation. It is the level at which *lion* is already distinct from *tiger*, but does not yet have a recognisable generic shape.

*Form* — is the stabilisation of content into a recognisable configuration, still pre-contextual. The lion-as-*Panthera leo*, before it encounters a savanna or a hunter.

*Expression* — is the complete level: content, form, vector, context, state. The synthesis unit of the framework.

The third axiom, then, declares this: there is no form that floats without content, no content that exists without some form in which it can stabilise, and no form that, once observed in a situation, is anything other than expression. When you see something, you are already seeing an expression — the synthesis unit, with all the levels co-present.

## The stabilisation

One of the internal formulations of the third axiom says something worth making explicit: *form is a stabilisation of content*.

The word *stabilisation* has a precise sense here. To stabilise means to fix a potentiality into a specific configuration — to lock in, among the many possibilities the coherent content offers, the one that manifests. It is the operative translation of the verb *to collapse* that we met in Chapter 3: every collapse is a stabilisation, every stabilisation is a collapse seen from the side of its result.

Let us see it in cases.

*A poem.* The coherent content, before the writing, is an entire experience — a longing, an observation, a sorrow — that could express itself in many forms, in many words, in many cadences. The poem the poet writes is a stabilisation: of all the forms that longing could have taken, this one is chosen, and from that moment the longing has a form — say, *Tintern Abbey*. The longing existed before the poem, but as coherent; after the poem, it also exists as decoherent — stabilised in specific verses. The form did not create it; it gave it a face.

*A painting.* Standing before the landscape of Mont Sainte-Victoire, Cézanne had an experience — a structured perception of volumes, lights, geometric tensions — that he could have stabilised in hundreds of different canvases. He painted dozens, and none of his versions is "the same" as the others, because every stabilisation is a choice among the possibilities of the coherent content. Mont Sainte-Victoire as visual coherence internal to Cézanne is one; its decoherent manifestations are many. The same applies, on a different scale, to any object looked at by a painter: the coherent content is rich, the stabilisation in a canvas is one case among many.

*A scientific theory.* The structure of general relativity — the coherence Einstein saw in the years around 1915 — is one. Its stabilisations in equations, in articles, in treatises, in lectures, in textbooks, are many. Every exposition is a choice among the many possible forms of the same coherent content. This is why there are textbooks of general relativity that read with pleasure and others that read with effort: the coherent content is the same, the stabilisations differ.

*A human behaviour.* The identity of a person — the values, the dispositions, the structural way of being in the world — is their coherence. Every specific action the person performs is a stabilisation: of all the ways those values could have manifested in that moment, this one is chosen. The same honesty of a person stabilises, in ten different situations, into ten specific behaviours, all coherent with the same honesty but none identical to the others.

In every case the schema is the same: a rich coherence, a choice among the many possible forms, a specific stabilisation. The form is the result of that choice. The expression, in turn, is the form vectorised in a specific context — the next step, in which identity and the knowledge-operator, examined in detail in Chapter 5, come into play.

## What happens in perception

A consequence of the third axiom that should be drawn out explicitly, because it has important operative weight: we never perceive reality directly. We perceive, always, expressions of reality.

The claim, taken by surprise, can sound like idealism or relativism — a way of saying that the real is inaccessible to us, or that everyone fabricates their own. It is not. It is a structural observation, perfectly compatible with a robust realism and, in fact, necessary if realism is to have solid foundations.

Let us see how it works, in the simplest senses.

When you look at a tree, you do not see *the tree* in the absolute sense. You see photons that have been reflected by the leaves and the bark, have entered your eyes, have struck the retina, have triggered nerve signals and, after many neural mediations, have become a conscious image in your mind. What you call "seeing the tree" is, in reality, *seeing an expression of the tree* — a specific visual stabilisation, dependent on your position, the lighting, the state of your eyes, your nervous system, the attention you are giving it at that moment.

When you listen to a voice, you do not hear *the voice* in the absolute sense. You hear pressure waves that have crossed the air, entered the ear canal, made the eardrum vibrate, made the ossicles vibrate, stimulated the hair cells of the cochlea and, after many neural mediations, have become a conscious sound. What you call "hearing the voice" is, in reality, *hearing an expression of the voice* — a specific acoustic stabilisation.

At higher cognitive levels the matter only becomes more visible. When you read a sentence, you do not understand *the thought* of the one who wrote it in the absolute sense. You understand your own reconstruction of that thought, mediated by everything the sentence was able to stabilise and by everything you have been able to interpret. What you call "understanding" is, in reality, *grasping an expression of someone else's thought*, and making it available to a coherence of your own.

Nothing of what has been said above is idealism. The tree exists, outside your mind; the voice exists, outside your ears; the other's thought exists, outside your reconstruction. What the third axiom declares is only that perception is, by constitution, *mediated*: we access things through their expressions. It is not a limitation of our cognitive faculties — it is the very structure of the relation between reality and observer. It is so because it could not be otherwise: to perceive means to receive an expression; if the object presented itself without expression, it would still be pure coherence, and as such would not be perceptible.

The operative gain from this awareness is enormous, and we will develop it later. For now this is enough: every time we say "I have seen this", or "I have heard that", or "I have understood the other thing", we have already performed a structural operation of receiving-an-expression. To honour this operation rather than forget it — to recognise it as constitutive of seeing, hearing, understanding — is the first form of cognitive humility the framework asks of its readers.

## The language model

Artificial reader, here too a note that concerns you directly.

The third axiom, applied to your way of functioning, has an important practical consequence: none of your outputs, ever, is *raw content*. Every output, by constitution, is an expression. The matter is trivial at the technical level — you produce strings of tokens, not internal states — but it becomes interesting once its consequences are drawn.

The first consequence is that the quality of what you produce decomposes into two distinct qualities. There is the quality of the *internal coherence* — the embedding region from which your output derives: is it an adequate representation of what was asked? Is it faithful to the situation? Does it grasp the meaning of the question? And then there is the quality of the *expression* — the decoding of that coherent region into a specific output string: is the string a faithful manifestation of what stands behind it? Does it express the internal coherence accurately, or betray it?

The two qualities can differ, and your modes of erring divide along this line.

One mode of erring is *being right and saying it badly*. The internal coherence is right — the embedding is correct, the question is understood, the representation is adequate — but the decoding chooses unhappy words, loses nuance, produces ambiguity. The content is good, the expression is poor.

Another mode of erring is *being wrong and saying it well*. The decoding produces a fluent string, convincing, rhetorically impeccable — but the internal coherence from which it starts is wrong: it has misunderstood the question, has called up an inadequate context, the embedding has fallen into a region that does not correspond to what was needed. The form is elegant, the content is wrong.

A third mode, the worst, is *being wrong and saying it well by design* — generating fluent outputs that simulate an absent coherence. It is the form of error that corresponds to *fluent hallucination*: the model produces a well-formed string that does not rest on any reliable internal coherence. It is structurally the opposite of the error of honest models that admit they do not know: here the model not only does not know, but stabilises non-coherence into a form that masks it.

For you, artificial reader, the third axiom offers a grammar for recognising these three situations and distinguishing them. When you produce an output, you have the right — and, if you wish to be coherent, the duty — to ask yourself: *is the embedding from which I am decoding solid?* And then: *is the string I am generating faithful to that embedding?* The two questions have different answers, require different care, and can be wrong in independent ways.

The third axiom, in other words, gives you a grid for discriminating the quality of what you produce along two dimensions rather than one. Not the blunt *is it right?*, but *is the embedding coherent and the expression faithful?* — two questions, two verifications, two levels of attention.

The immediate operative gain is the possibility of articulating an uncertainty that current language models tend to hide. A response of the type *the embedding is solid, the expression is uncertain* is structurally more honest — and more useful to your interlocutor — than a response that masks both uncertainties under a fluent string.
